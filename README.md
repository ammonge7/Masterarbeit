Background:
Excess sugar consumption contributes to obesity, cardiovascular disease, and diabetes. Taxes on sugar-sweetened beverages are a cost-effective policy tool, but their success is dependent on public acceptability. This is particularly relevant in Latin America, where excess sugar consumption and sugar-sweetened beverage consumption are high, and policy implementation is varied. Online comment sections through social media offer accessible data on public opinion, though large-scale qualitative analysis is resource-intensive. Large language models (LLMs) offer a potential automated solution, but currently lack more robust pipelines to analyse online comments.
Methods:
This study evaluated whether LLMs can replicate human qualitative coding of online discourse on sugar, non-nutritive sweeteners, and related policies. Focusing on Latin America, comments were collected from Facebook news pages in Peru (with a sugar-sweetened beverage tax) and Colombia (without a tax). A subset of the data (10%) was coded by a human coder for comparison.
Results:
LLM coding showed high percentage agreement with human coding (generally >70%), but Cohen’s kappa indicated poor-to-moderate reliability (κ = 0.00–0.72), likely due to imbalanced category distributions. A key difference emerged in expressions of distrust: Colombian comments voiced more distrust in government, while Peruvian comments emphasised distrust in their health system.
Conclusion:
LLMs show potential for supporting qualitative analysis of large-scale online data in Latin American contexts while using less time and resources, but require refinement to improve reliability. Further testing on wider data sets or exploring inductive coding approaches may provide a deeper understanding both the application of LLMs in research settings as well as the nuances of public perceptions of health policy.
